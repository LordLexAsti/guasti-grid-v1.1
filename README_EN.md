# guasti-grid-v1.1

YAML/Markdown formalization of an architecture of responsibility in lethal decision systems.

## Overview

This repository proposes a grammar of responsibility for lethal systems assisted by software, algorithms, or artificial intelligence. Its guiding thesis is clear: a machine may participate in a causal chain, but it cannot bear moral, political, or legal responsibility. That responsibility remains human and institutional.

The aim of the project is to prevent a lethal act from being dissolved into an opaque technical chain in which everyone has supposedly done only "a small part" and no one fully answers for the death produced. Its concern is therefore not to moralize technology in the abstract, but to resist a modern form of organized irresponsibility: one in which the sophistication of devices, interfaces, procedures, and chains of command makes the author of the act diffuse, replaceable, and practically untraceable.

In this perspective, the project advances a controversial but arguable proposition: when a lethal system becomes so opaque, so fast, or so distributed that it prevents us from clearly identifying who could understand, interrupt, or redirect the action, the problem is not merely technical. It becomes institutional, legal, and philosophical.

## Useful Philosophical Version

### Ultra-short summary

A machine cannot be responsible for a lethal act; only human beings and institutions can be. The project therefore proposes a method for examining who really decides, who could refuse, who must answer for the act, and who owes repair to victims.

### The starting problem

When a weapon system assisted by software or artificial intelligence participates in a decision to kill, one question returns immediately: who answers for the death?

The project begins from a sober observation: the more technical, fast, distributed, and opaque a system becomes, the easier it is for human actors to take cover behind it. The risk is not only error. The risk is also the apparent disappearance of the author, as though technical complexity itself could erase responsibility.

### The central idea

The core of the project is to reject the dissolution of responsibility into technique. A machine may recommend, sort, signal, accelerate, or execute an instruction. It cannot answer for itself before victims, a court, a political community, or humanity more broadly.

The repository therefore proposes that we systematically trace the lethal act back to its human infrastructure:
- the funder,
- the designer,
- the government,
- the command structure,
- the operator,
- and the victims as the center of repair.

This tracing exercise is not meant to multiply guilty parties arbitrarily. On the contrary, it is meant to prevent the opposite fiction: the idea that the accumulation of technical mediations is enough to make the obligation to answer disappear. The more mediated a lethal decision is, the more explicit the demand for imputability must become. If no one is clearly in a position to answer, then responsibility has not vanished; rather, the system has been built in such a way as to make responsibility unreadable.

## Essential conceptual points

### 1. The machine is not a responsible subject

The project argues that an AI system is not a moral node. It may be causally involved, but it cannot answer in the strong sense, be morally judged, or assume a political debt toward victims.

### 2. Responsibility requires real capacity

The decisive question is not merely: is there a human in the loop?

The real question is: could that human actually say no?

The project distinguishes between theoretical capacity and real capacity. If an operator acts under extreme pressure, through an opaque interface, without access to raw data, without time to deliberate, and without institutional protection in case of refusal, then that operator's responsibility may be only apparent. In that case, responsibility must move upward to the higher levels that designed, imposed, or organized the situation.

### 3. A critique of cosmetic responsibility

The repository criticizes the idea that the mere presence of a human being is enough to guarantee an authentically human decision. A human may be "in the loop" while possessing no real power at all. In that case, the human becomes a legitimating buffer for a system they do not actually control.

### 4. Decision and repair are two distinct chains

The project distinguishes between:
- the chain of decision, which leads to the act,
- the chain of repair, which organizes truth, recognition, compensation, and reform after the act.

This distinction helps prevent responsibility from being reduced to the mere designation of a culprit. It also includes the way institutions must answer to victims.

## Argumentative position

This project advances an assertive thesis, but not an irrational one: a lethal decision is politically admissible only if one can identify, in an intelligible and verifiable way, the human and institutional agents who made its occurrence possible, who could have interrupted it, and who must answer for it afterward.

The point is not to deny technical complexity, nor to pretend that contemporary chains of command are simple. The point is to insist that no degree of complexity, however real, is enough to abolish the elementary demand for responsibility. A complexity that structurally prevents us from knowing who answers for death is not neutral: it already produces a political effect. It shifts the burden of the act into the fog of organization.

In other words, the project does not say: "technology is bad because it is technology." It says: a lethal apparatus becomes philosophically and politically suspect when it makes imputability harder to establish than the action itself. In a state governed by law—or in any order that still claims to distinguish legitimate violence from arbitrary violence—this asymmetry should be treated as a warning sign.

## Critical reading

### Strengths

- The project clearly refuses the dilution of responsibility into technical complexity.
- It introduces a strong distinction between formal capacity and real capacity.
- It takes seriously the role of interface design, time, information, and hierarchy in decision-making.
- It usefully distinguishes the production of the act from obligations of repair.
- It formalizes its claims, which makes disagreement more visible and more discussable.

### Points of fragility

- Several axioms are normative rather than strictly demonstrated.
- The proposed scores and thresholds may create an appearance of exact measurement even though they should be read as heuristic tools.
- The actor categories sometimes simplify more hybrid institutional realities.
- The idea of responsibility before "humanity" is morally strong but politically and legally contestable.
- The style of formalization may be attacked as a codification of moral intuitions rather than a demonstration in the strong sense.

## Hostile objections and replies

### Objection 1 — "You are anthropomorphizing responsibility and refusing to see real technical autonomy."

**Reply.** The project does not deny the functional autonomy of a system. It distinguishes two planes: operational autonomy and normative responsibility. The fact that a system selects, classifies, or engages a target with a high degree of autonomy is not enough to make it a responsible subject. Technical autonomy describes a mode of execution; it does not by itself create a moral, legal, or political subject.

### Objection 2 — "Your model is activist: you have hidden a political conclusion inside symbols."

**Reply.** This objection is serious and should be taken seriously. Yes, the model rests on explicit normative choices. But that is precisely the value of formalization: it makes presuppositions visible instead of allowing them to operate implicitly inside industrial, bureaucratic, or military discourses that present themselves as neutral. The repository does not eliminate disagreement; it makes disagreement examinable.

### Objection 3 — "In practice, responsibility is too distributed to be properly assigned."

**Reply.** The project recognizes that distribution; it does not deny it. What it rejects is the illegitimate leap from "causality is distributed" to "responsibility becomes untraceable." A plurality of contributors does not imply an absence of responsible agents. It requires, on the contrary, a more demanding cartography of decision, constraint, and control.

### Objection 4 — "The operator is still responsible: they either press the button or they do not."

**Reply.** That formula is solid only if one can show that the operator had a real capacity to refuse. If time is missing, if the interface nudges, if information is locked away, if hierarchy punishes doubt, then the operator may be no more than a terminal point of imputation. The project does not automatically exonerate the operator; it refuses to let the operator serve as a convenient receptacle for responsibility produced elsewhere.

### Objection 5 — "Your metrics create an illusion of scientificity."

**Reply.** The risk exists and should be stated plainly. The project's metrics should be understood as heuristic tools for comparison and audit, not as natural measures of morality. Their usefulness lies not in illusory precision, but in their capacity to make discussable dimensions that vague discourse often leaves in the dark.

### Objection 6 — "Humanity as an audience is an abstraction without a stable institution."

**Reply.** This is partly true. But the absence of a perfectly unified world institution does not abolish all transnational normativity. International humanitarian law, courts, commissions, the press, NGOs, alliances, and historical precedents already constitute—imperfectly—forms of interpellation beyond the national frame alone. The project turns that moral intuition into an analytical demand.

### Objection 7 — "Your model is too simple for real architectures."

**Reply.** Every model simplifies. The relevant question is therefore not whether it simplifies, but whether it simplifies fruitfully. Here, the reduction to a limited number of poles is intended to avoid the opposite effect: a proliferation of details that would make structured criticism impossible. A map is not the territory; it may nevertheless be necessary in order not to get lost in the territory.

## Conditions of argumentative robustness

To be defended academically, this project should be read as a proposal for normative and institutional clarification, not as a moral physics of automated war. Its scores are analytical instruments, its axioms are explicit theoretical commitments, and its categories are simplifications oriented toward critical audit.

Its strength therefore does not depend on a claim to infallibility. It depends on a more sober and robust claim: a lethal system that kills faster than it allows us to establish who ought to answer for the death is, in itself, a major political problem.

## Structured map of the YAML

### 0. Reference table
Lexicon of actors, responsibilities, repairs, and audiences.

### 1. Formal universe
Defines the basic sets: actors, types of responsibility, forms of repair, and the audiences to whom actors must answer.

### 2. Nodes
Describes actors as bearers of responsibilities, obligations of repair, and a real capacity to refuse or redirect action.

### 3. Machine as non-responsible
States the exclusion of AI as a morally or legally responsible subject.

### 4. Responsibility predicate
Defines responsibility through three conditions: addressability, imputability, and real capacity.

### 5. Axioms
Structures the whole framework: non-delegation to AI, impossibility of a lethal act without a human responsible party, double audience, capacity threshold, and internal/external anchoring of the duty to answer.

### 6. Graphs
Distinguishes the decision graph from the repair graph.

### 7. Column of the act and completeness
Attempts to assess whether the human chain of responsibility is complete or artificially truncated.

### 8. Real operator capacity
Analyzes time, information, hierarchy, interface, and rules of engagement as conditions for a genuinely human decision.

### 9. Interface quality
Shows that interface design concretely distributes the power to understand, hesitate, refuse, or escalate.

### 10. Operational tempo
Thinks through the degradation of responsibility guarantees according to the level of operational intensity, without ever allowing the complete disappearance of a responsible subject.

- who makes the act possible;
- who can understand, interrupt, or redirect it;
- who must answer for it;
- and who owes repair to victims.

The project does not seek to moralize technology in the abstract. ## Defensive FAQ for aggressive criticism

### 1. Isn't this project simply an anti-AI manifesto?
No. The project does not rest on a general condemnation of AI. It addresses a more precise problem: the conditions under which a lethal decision remains politically, morally, and legally imputable. Its central point is not "technology is bad," but "technology must not be used to erase responsibility."

### 2. Why insist that a machine cannot be responsible?
Because normative responsibility cannot be reduced to causal implication. To be responsible is to be answerable: to be questioned, judged, and called to account. A machine can produce an effect; it cannot assume a moral, legal, or political debt toward victims.

### 3. But if a system acts autonomously, isn't it logical to assign it some responsibility?
No, not in the strong sense. Technical autonomy describes a mode of execution. It does not by itself create a moral or legal subject. Confusing functional autonomy with normative responsibility turns an operational property into a political personality.

### 4. Doesn't your critique underestimate the real complexity of contemporary chains of decision?
On the contrary. The project begins from that complexity. It simply argues that complexity cannot serve as an alibi for irresponsibility. The more distributed the chain, the more rigorously one must map decision, constraint, and control.

### 5. Why challenge the notion of "human in the loop"?
The project does not reject it outright; it rejects its use as an automatic guarantee. The presence of a human being is not enough. What matters is whether that human has a real capacity to understand, hesitate, refuse, or escalate the decision without being crushed by the structure around them.

### 6. Aren't you trying to excuse the operator and blame higher levels instead?
No. The project does not excuse the operator by default. It simply refuses to let the operator become a convenient container for responsibility generated elsewhere. If the operator had real power and sufficient information, responsibility remains. If that power was fictive, the chain must be traced upward.

### 7. Isn't your model activist rather than scientific?
The project is explicitly normative. It does not claim to be a hard science of morality. Its strength lies precisely in making its presuppositions explicit, rather than hiding them in bureaucratic, strategic, or industrial language that falsely presents itself as neutral. This is a critical formalization, not a naturalization of normativity.

### 8. Don't your scores and metrics create an illusion of scientific rigor?
That risk exists and should be acknowledged. These metrics should be read as heuristic tools for audit and comparison, not as absolute measures of moral truth. Their function is to make certain critical dimensions visible: opacity, real capacity, traceability, and reparability.

### 9. Why include interface quality in a problem of responsibility?
Because the interface is not neutral. It conditions access to information, visibility of uncertainty, symmetry of options, time for reflection, and possibilities for escalation. In that sense, design concretely distributes moral and political power.

### 10. Doesn't responsibility always end up dissolving into distributed causality?
No. Distributed causality does not mean that there are no responsible parties left. It means responsibility must be thought in a structured, plural, and layered way. The project rejects the slide from "many contributors" to "therefore no one is really responsible."

### 11. Why include funders, designers, and governments if they do not make the terminal decision?
Because a lethal act does not begin with the final gesture. It results from earlier decisions: funding, technical architecture, doctrine, rules of engagement, interface configuration, and authorized levels of autonomy. Limiting responsibility to the final operator often erases the political and institutional conditions that made the act possible.

### 12. Isn't responsibility before "humanity" too abstract to be useful?
It is ambitious, and it can be contested. But it is not empty. International humanitarian law, courts, historical precedents, commissions of inquiry, NGOs, media, and transnational forms of public scrutiny show that there are already scenes of responsibility that exceed the national frame alone.

### 13. Isn't your model too simple to describe real architectures?
Every model simplifies. The relevant question is whether the simplification is useful. Here, the aim is not to reproduce the full complexity of reality, but to make visible what that complexity often conceals: who knew, who decided, who could refuse, and who owes repair.

### 14. What is this project concretely useful for?
It provides an analytical grid for auditing contemporary lethal architectures. It helps formulate precise questions about:
- the operator's real capacity;
- interface quality;
- decision traceability;
- the distribution of responsibility;
- obligations of repair.

Its usefulness is institutional as much as intellectual: it helps prevent sophisticated systems from producing structural irresponsibility.

### 15. In one sentence, what is the core of your position?
A lethal decision is politically defensible only if one can identify, in an intelligible and verifiable way, the human beings and institutions that made it possible, could have interrupted it, and must answer for it afterward.
